When supporting high school science students with learning disabilities, the focus should be on foundational "big ideas" rather than rote memorization of technical jargon. Prioritizing conceptual understanding, visual processing, and real-world relevance allows students to grasp core mechanics without becoming overwhelmed by dense text.

Here are the essential core concepts across the major science disciplines, framed for accessible mastery:

1. Biology: The "Systems" Approach
• The Cell as a Factory: Focus on the cell as the basic unit of life. Students should understand that every living thing is made of cells and that these cells perform specific jobs (like producing energy, making proteins, or managing waste) just like a factory.
• Energy Flow (Photosynthesis & Respiration): Rather than memorizing every chemical formula, focus on the "input and output": Plants take in sunlight to make energy, and animals break down food to release energy.
• DNA as Instructions: Conceptualize DNA not as a complex molecule, but as the "instruction manual" for building and operating an organism.

2. Chemistry: The "Building Blocks" Approach
• Matter is Made of Particles: The most essential concept is that everything is made of tiny atoms and molecules that are constantly moving. Understanding that temperature is just a measurement of how fast these particles move is a vital breakthrough.
• The Periodic Table as a Menu: Instead of memorizing elements, teach students to see the Periodic Table as a "map" that organizes materials based on how they behave.
• Chemical Reactions (Mixing & Changing): Understand that chemical reactions are simply the rearranging of atoms to form new substances, like a puzzle being taken apart and put together differently.

3. Physics: The "Mechanics" Approach
• Forces and Motion: Focus on the relationship between force and change. If an object is sitting still, it needs a "push or pull" (force) to move. If it is moving, it needs a force to stop or change direction.
• Energy Conservation: The core idea here is that energy never disappears; it just changes form (e.g., electrical energy turning into light in a bulb, or potential energy turning into motion).

4. Earth/Environmental Science: The "Cycle" Approach
• Interconnected Systems: Earth is a set of interacting systems (water, air, land, and life). Students should master how changing one part (like adding heat to the atmosphere) causes a ripple effect in the others.
• The Water/Carbon Cycle: Focus on the cyclical nature of these resources—they are constantly being recycled, not created or destroyed.
Strategies for Mastery
• Visual-First Learning: Use isometric diagrams and 3D models to represent these concepts, as these reduce the reliance on abstract linguistic processing.
• Chunking: Break large units (like "Cell Biology") into smaller, manageable "jobs" (e.g., "The Powerhouse," "The Gatekeeper," "The Blueprint") to make the information less daunting.
• Concrete Examples: Always tie these abstract concepts to daily life—like how a phone battery (Physics/Chemistry) works or how the body fights a cold (Biology).
